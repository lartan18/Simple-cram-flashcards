What are the three components of the CIA triad?\CIA stands for Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability. Confidentiality ensures only authorized principals (users or processes) can read data; Integrity ensures only authorized principals can modify data; and Availability ensures systems and data are accessible to authorized users when needed. $ What is the distinction between Privacy and Confidentiality?\Privacy is a principal's right to control their own personal information and how it is collected or used. Confidentiality is an obligation or duty of an organization to protect the information entrusted to them by others. $ What is Non-Repudiation?\Non-Repudiation is a security property where a principal cannot later deny having performed a specific action or transaction. It is typically achieved through digital signatures and audit logs that provide evidence of the action. $ What does Accountability mean in a security context?\Accountability is the ability to uniquely trace every action within a system back to a specific principal. This relies on identification, authentication, and the creation of audit trails or logs. $ What is the difference between a Principal and an Identity?\A principal is any entity (user, process, or device) capable of acting within a system. An identity is a label or identifier, such as a username or ID number, used to distinguish one principal from another. $ What is the difference between a trusted and a trustworthy system?\A trusted system is one that is in a position to break a specific security policy if it fails (it is "trusted" with power). A trustworthy system is one that is verified or proven to consistently operate correctly and not fail. $ What is the Penetrate and Patch mindset?\It is the practice of developing software without prioritizing security, and then attempting to fix vulnerabilities only after they are discovered in production. This is discouraged in favor of a "secure by design" approach. $ What is the Principle of Least Privilege?\A security principle stating that every principal (user or process) must be granted only the absolute minimum level of access and permissions required to perform its function, and only for the duration necessary. $ What is Defense in Depth?\The implementation of multiple, redundant security layers (such as firewalls, encryption, and access controls). If one layer fails, subsequent layers are in place to prevent a total system compromise. $ What is the Weakest Link principle?\The concept that a system's overall security is only as strong as its most vulnerable component. Security efforts should be prioritized on the areas most likely to be exploited. $ What is the principle of Compartmentalization?\The practice of isolating system components or data into distinct "compartments" or zones. This ensures that if an attacker compromises one part of the system, they cannot easily move to or access other parts. $ What is the principle of Reluctance to Trust?\A design mindset that assumes all external inputs, third-party code libraries, and remote actors are potentially malicious until their integrity and identity are verified. $ What is the principle of Keeping it Simple?\The strategy of reducing system complexity. Smaller, simpler systems have a reduced "attack surface" (fewer entry points for an attacker) and are easier to verify for security. $ What is Security by Obscurity?\The discouraged practice of relying entirely on the secrecy of a system's internal design or implementation details (like a hidden URL) as the primary means of protection. $ What is the European CRA?\The Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) is a regulation that requires manufacturers to implement security lifecycle management and perform risk assessments for any product containing digital components. $ What is an SBOM?\SBOM stands for Software Bill of Materials. It is a comprehensive, machine-readable inventory of all third-party components, libraries, and dependencies used to build a software product, essential for tracking vulnerabilities. $ What is Kerckhoffs' Principle?\The principle that a cryptographic system should be secure even if everything about it is public knowledge, provided that the specific secret key remains hidden. $ What is a zero-day exploit?\An exploit that targets a software vulnerability for which no patch or fix exists because the software vendor is not yet aware that the flaw exists. $ What is the difference between a Threat and a Vulnerability?\A vulnerability is a flaw or weakness in a system's design or code. A threat is a potential actor or event (like a hacker or a system crash) that could exploit that vulnerability to cause harm.
