What are the three components of the CIA triad?\CIA stands for Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability. Confidentiality ensures only authorized principals can read data; Integrity ensures only authorized principals can modify data; and Availability ensures systems and data are accessible to authorized users when needed.$
What is the distinction between Privacy and Confidentiality?\Privacy is a principal's right to control their own personal information and how it is collected. Confidentiality is the obligation of an organization to protect the information entrusted to them by others.$ What is Non-Repudiation?\Non-Repudiation is a security property where a principal cannot later deny having performed a specific action, typically achieved through digital signatures and audit logs that provide evidence of the transaction.$
What does Accountability mean in a security context?\Accountability is the ability to uniquely trace every action within a system back to a specific principal, relying on logging and audit trails.$ What is the difference between a Principal and an Identity?\A principal is any entity (user, process, or device) capable of acting within a system. An identity is a label or identifier, such as a username, used to distinguish one principal from another.$
What is the difference between a trusted and a trustworthy system?\A trusted system is one that has the power to break a security policy if it fails. A trustworthy system is one that is verified to consistently operate correctly and not fail.$ What is the Penetrate and Patch mindset?\It is the discouraged practice of fixing vulnerabilities only after they are discovered in production, rather than designing the system to be secure from the start.$
What is the Principle of Least Privilege?\A security principle stating that a principal must be granted only the absolute minimum level of access and permissions required to perform its function for the minimum necessary duration.$ What is Defense in Depth?\The implementation of multiple, redundant security layers so that if one defensive mechanism fails, others are in place to prevent a total system compromise.$
What is the Weakest Link principle?\The concept that a system's overall security is only as strong as its most vulnerable component, requiring engineers to focus efforts where failure is most likely.$ What is the principle of Compartmentalization?\The practice of isolating system components into distinct zones so that a compromise in one area does not allow an attacker to reach the rest of the system.$
What is the principle of Reluctance to Trust?\A design mindset that assumes all external inputs and third-party code are potentially malicious until verified.$ What is the principle of Keeping it Simple?\The strategy of reducing system complexity to minimize the attack surface and make security properties easier to verify.$
What is Security by Obscurity?\The discouraged practice of relying solely on the secrecy of implementation details, like a hidden URL, as the primary means of protection.$ What is the European CRA?\CRA stands for the Cyber Resilience Act, a regulation requiring manufacturers to implement risk assessments and security lifecycle management for digital products.$
What is an SBOM?\SBOM stands for Software Bill of Materials, which is a comprehensive inventory of all third-party components and libraries used to build a software product.$ What is Kerckhoffs' Principle?\The principle that a cryptographic system should be secure even if everything about it is public knowledge, provided the secret key remains hidden.$
What is a zero-day exploit?\An attack that targets a software vulnerability before the vendor is aware of it or has released a patch.$ What is the difference between a Threat and a Vulnerability?\A vulnerability is a flaw or weakness in a system's design or code. A threat is a potential actor or event (like a hacker or a system crash) that could exploit that vulnerability to cause harm.$
What are typical attack types mentioned in security fundamentals?\Typical attacks include Web defacement, Infiltration (control hijacking), Phishing, Data theft, Denial of Service (DoS), and Ransomware.$
